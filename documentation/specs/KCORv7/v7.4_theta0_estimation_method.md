# Theta0 Estimation Under Epidemic Waves: The Delta-Iteration Method (v7.4)

## The Problem

The KCOR estimator fits a gamma-frailty Gompertz model to observed cohort hazard data to recover
`theta0` — the frailty variance at enrollment. The marginal cohort hazard is:

$$h_\text{obs}(t) = \frac{k \cdot e^{\gamma t}}{1 + \theta_0 \cdot H_0^\text{eff}(t)}$$

where `H0_eff(t)` is the cumulative individual-level baseline hazard. During quiet periods,
`H0_eff(t) = H_gom(t)` — the pure Gompertz cumulative hazard. But during an epidemic wave,
`H0_eff` accumulates **faster** than `H_gom` because individuals face elevated mortality.

After the wave ends, `H0_eff(t)` carries a **permanent excess** over `H_gom(t)`:

$$H_0^\text{eff}(t) = H_\text{gom}(t) + \delta \quad \text{for all } t \geq t_\text{wave end}$$

The wave has **fast-forwarded** the population's frailty depletion clock. The surviving cohort
is systematically healthier than the pure Gompertz formula predicts.

### The Original Bug (pre-v7.4)

The original estimator fitted the same formula to all quiet windows — pre-wave and post-wave —
using the same time index `t` and the same `H_gom(t)`. But post-wave quiet points sit on a
**different branch** of the curve, shifted forward by `delta`. The optimizer compensated by
inflating `theta_hat`, producing **+14% bias** on post-wave windows.

---

## The Key Insight: Waves Are Time Gaps

An epidemic wave does not change the **shape** of the Gompertz-frailty curve. It only
**fast-forwards** the population along it by depositing extra cumulative hazard `delta`.

Therefore:
- All quiet windows — pre and post any number of waves — lie on the **same smooth curve**
- The wave creates a **jump** in `H0_eff` that can be measured from the data
- Once each jump `delta_i` is known, all quiet windows are internally consistent
- A single `theta` fits all of them perfectly

---

## Applicability: When Delta-Iteration Applies

**The delta-iteration method is only valid when `delta > 0`**, i.e., when the epidemic wave
caused net excess mortality in the cohort, fast-forwarding frailty depletion beyond the
Gompertz baseline.

This condition holds reliably for **unvaccinated cohorts (dose=0)**, where COVID waves
produced genuine excess mortality. It may fail for **vaccinated cohorts (dose ≥ 1)** where:

- The healthy-vaccinee selection effect suppresses observed hazard *below* the Gompertz
  baseline during and after the wave
- The net effect of the wave on `H0_eff` is zero or slightly negative relative to `H_gom`
- Forcing `delta ≥ 0` by clamping produces a biased `H0_eff` reconstruction, which then
  corrupts theta in all subsequent iterations

**Rule:** If `delta < 0` after the first reconstruction pass, the delta-iteration method is
**inapplicable** for that cohort. The estimator must fall back to the single-quiet-window
method (pre-wave window only), which is the v7.3 behavior. This is logged as
`DELTA_INAPPLICABLE` and does not indicate an error — it is the correct adaptive behavior.

---

## The Delta-Iteration Algorithm

### Parameters
- `k` — Gompertz baseline hazard at `t=0`, read exactly from data
- `theta0` — frailty variance at enrollment
- `delta_i` — excess cumulative hazard deposited by wave `i` (one per wave)

### Step 1: Read k Exactly

`k` is computed as the direct average of `h_obs(t)` over the first `k_anchor_weeks` weeks,
divided by the mean Gompertz factor `exp(γt)` over that window:

$$k = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{t=1}^{W} \frac{h_\text{obs}(t)}{e^{\gamma t}}$$

where `W = k_anchor_weeks`. **This is a direct data read — no optimization, no bounds,
no fitting.** All `k_anchor_weeks` data points are included in the subsequent theta fit;
they are not excluded from the fitting window. A bounded optimizer for k must never be
used; the `K_BOUND_HIT` warning is not a valid operating state.

### Step 2: Check Delta-Iteration Applicability

Before iterating, compute a provisional `delta` using the seed theta from the pre-wave
quiet window (Step 3a below, first pass). If `delta < 0`:

- Set `delta = 0`
- Log `DELTA_INAPPLICABLE`
- Skip to **Fallback: Single-Window Fit** below
- Do not continue iterating

### Step 3: Initial theta from Pre-Wave Quiet Window

Fit a single-parameter nonlinear least squares on the pre-wave quiet window only:

$$\hat{\theta}_0 = \arg\min_\theta \sum_{t \in W_0} \left[ h_\text{obs}(t) - \frac{k \cdot e^{\gamma t}}{1 + \theta \cdot H_\text{gom}(t)} \right]^2$$

This gives a seed theta (~1% error) to initialize the iteration.

### Step 4: Iterate to Convergence

Repeat the following until `theta` converges (typically 2–3 iterations):

#### 4a: Reconstruct H0_eff from h_obs

Using the gamma-frailty inversion, reconstruct the individual-level cumulative hazard
from the observed cohort hazard:

$$h_0^\text{eff}(t) = h_\text{obs}(t) \cdot \left(1 + \theta \cdot H_0^\text{eff}(t)\right)$$
$$H_0^\text{eff}(t+1) = H_0^\text{eff}(t) + h_0^\text{eff}(t)$$

starting from `H0_eff(0) = 0`. This reconstruction passes through **all weeks** —
quiet and wave alike — with no special cases.

#### 4b: Check Delta Sign (first iteration only)

At the end of the first reconstruction pass, compute each wave's provisional delta:

$$\delta_i = H_0^\text{eff}(t_i) - H_\text{gom}(t_i)$$

If any `delta_i < 0`, stop immediately: log `DELTA_INAPPLICABLE` and fall back to the
single-window method. Do not clamp and continue — a clamped negative delta produces
a systematically wrong `H0_eff` for all post-wave time points, corrupting theta.

#### 4c: Compute Each Wave's Delta

For wave `i` with known end time `t_i`:

$$\delta_i = H_0^\text{eff}(t_i) - H_\text{gom}(t_i)$$

This is a **direct subtraction** — not a fit. For subsequent waves, deltas accumulate:

$$\Delta(t) = \sum_{i : t_i \leq t} \delta_i$$

#### 4d: Refit theta on ALL Quiet Windows

With `delta_i` values known, fit a single parameter `theta` to all quiet windows jointly:

$$\hat{\theta} = \arg\min_\theta \sum_{t \in \bigcup_j W_j} \left[ h_\text{obs}(t) - \frac{k \cdot e^{\gamma t}}{1 + \theta \cdot (H_\text{gom}(t) + \Delta(t))} \right]^2$$

where `W_j` are all quiet windows and `Delta(t)` is the accumulated delta up to time `t`.

---

## Fallback: Single-Window Fit

Used when `DELTA_INAPPLICABLE` is triggered. Fit theta using the pre-wave quiet window
only, with no delta correction:

$$\hat{\theta} = \arg\min_\theta \sum_{t \in W_0} \left[ h_\text{obs}(t) - \frac{k \cdot e^{\gamma t}}{1 + \theta \cdot H_\text{gom}(t)} \right]^2$$

This is identical to the v7.3 estimator. It is unbiased provided the pre-wave window has
sufficient deaths (governed by the `min_quiet_deaths` identifiability check). Post-wave
quiet windows are excluded from the fit for this cohort, since without a valid delta they
would reintroduce the original +14% bias.

---

## Properties

| Property | Delta-Iteration Path | Fallback Path |
|---|---|---|
| Applicable when | delta > 0 (dose=0, exposed cohorts) | delta ≤ 0 (vaccinated/selected cohorts) |
| Free parameters | 1 (`theta` only) | 1 (`theta` only) |
| k estimation | Direct read from first `k_anchor_weeks` | Same |
| Parameters computed analytically | N (`delta_i` per wave) | 0 |
| Iterations to convergence | 2–3 | 1 (single fit) |
| Bias with perfect data | < 0.01% | ~0% pre-wave; post-wave windows excluded |
| Requires wave timing knowledge | Yes | Yes (to exclude post-wave windows) |
| Handles N waves | Yes — one delta per wave | N/A |
| Log message | (none, normal path) | `DELTA_INAPPLICABLE` |

---

## Generalization to N Waves

For a study period with N epidemic waves at known calendar intervals `[ws_i, we_i]`:

1. `k` = direct average over first `k_anchor_weeks` — exact, no optimization
2. Quiet windows: all time outside `[ws_i, we_i]` for all i
3. Check delta sign after first reconstruction pass; fall back if negative
4. One `delta_i` per wave, read from reconstructed `H0_eff`
5. Single `theta` fit to all quiet windows with accumulated deltas applied

---

## Why the Original Method Was Biased

The original KCOR estimator used `H_gom(t)` for all windows including post-wave quiet windows.
Post-wave quiet points have `H0_eff(t) = H_gom(t) + delta`, so the denominator is **too small**
in the model, making the predicted hazard **too high**. The optimizer inflates `theta` to bring
the predicted curve down to match the data — producing the observed upward bias.

The delta-iteration method corrects this for cohorts where `delta > 0`. For cohorts where
the wave produced no net excess hazard (delta ≤ 0), no correction is needed and the
fallback single-window fit is both correct and unbiased.

## Limitations and Interpretation of Delta in Real Data

### What delta actually absorbs

In real registry data, delta absorbs **any deviation** between the true `H0_eff(t)` and the
assumed `H_gom(t)` over the non-quiet interval. This includes:

1. **Wave effects** — the intended signal: frailty-accelerated depletion from epidemic
   mortality. Dominant contribution for unvaccinated cohorts.

2. **Model misspecification** — if the true background hazard deviates from Gompertz
   (seasonal variation, cohort aging effects, changes in healthcare), delta absorbs the drift.

3. **Baseline drift** — slow secular trends in mortality over multi-year follow-up.

4. **Misclassified wave boundaries** — wave mortality leaking into quiet windows
   contaminates the theta fit.

### Diagnostic flags

- **`DELTA_INAPPLICABLE`** — delta ≤ 0 after first reconstruction pass; fallback path used.
  Expected and normal for vaccinated cohorts. Not an error.

- **Implausibly large delta** (larger than the entire pre-wave `H_gom`) — diagnostic flag
  for model misspecification or incorrect wave boundary placement.

- **Multiple deltas across waves** — if the model is well-specified, the ratio of delta_i
  to wave duration and multiplier should be roughly consistent across waves of similar severity.

### Summary

Delta is best described as:

> *The cumulative deviation of the true individual-level hazard from the assumed
> Gompertz baseline, integrated over the non-quiet interval. In a well-specified model
> with clearly identified epidemic waves on unvaccinated cohorts, this is dominated by
> wave-accelerated frailty depletion. For vaccinated or otherwise selected cohorts where
> the wave did not produce net excess hazard, delta is inapplicable and the single-window
> fallback is used instead.*

## Identifiability: When Does the Estimator Work Well?

Empirical sweep across 400 parameter combinations (20 mortality rates × 20 theta values,
two epidemic waves, 104-week follow-up) reveals a clear identifiability boundary.

### Results summary

| Follow-up | Worst-case error | Problem region |
|---|---|---|
| 2 years (104 weeks) | 4.93% | mortality < 3%/yr AND theta > 10 |
| 3 years (156 weeks) | 3.49% | mortality < 2%/yr AND theta > 12 |
| 4 years (208 weeks) | 2.14% | mortality < 2%/yr AND theta > 15 |

Outside the problem corner, errors are consistently **< 0.5%** regardless of follow-up.

### Root cause: geometric curvature

The estimator requires the h(t) curve to have enough **curvature** over the observation
window for the optimizer to distinguish theta from k. The total curvature is governed by:

```
curvature ∝ annual_mortality × n_years × theta × gamma
```

When this product is small — low mortality, short follow-up, small theta — the h(t) curve
is nearly flat and k and theta become nearly collinear.

### Practical implication

For typical all-cause mortality cohorts (≥ 4%/yr) with theta in the range 1–10 and
2+ years of follow-up, the estimator is well-identified with errors well under 1%.
The Czech Republic registry data (≈6%/yr, 2+ year follow-up) falls comfortably in
the well-identified zone.

For low-mortality cohorts or very short follow-up, the identifiability limit is a
fundamental geometric property of the model — not a limitation of the algorithm —
and cannot be overcome by any estimator without additional assumptions.
