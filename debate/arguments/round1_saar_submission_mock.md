# Round 1 Saar Submission: US mRNA Net Mortality Through 2022

Debate question: In the United States, did the mRNA COVID vaccines likely net save lives or cost lives through the end of 2022?

Position: the more defensible answer is that mRNA vaccination likely net saved lives through the end of 2022, especially in the age groups that drove COVID mortality. The negative side raises real confounding issues, but it has not yet produced a cleaner US counterfactual that overturns the strongest direct efficacy evidence.

## Proposed Judge Categories

1. Direct severe-COVID efficacy: what do the highest-quality randomized and near-randomized data show?
2. Observational validity: do the main death studies survive HVE, NCACM, prior-infection, and timing objections?
3. Population-level fingerprint: do real-world mortality patterns look like large vaccine benefit or not?
4. US-specific reconciliation: can the opposing side quantitatively reconcile persistent US mortality elevation with its net-harm claim?
5. Counterfactual strength: which side gives the cleaner, more falsifiable explanation of what would have happened without vaccination?

## Claim 1: The Best Direct Evidence Still Supports Meaningful Protection Against Severe COVID

The strongest evidence is not the weakest observational cohort. It is the combination of the randomized trials and the early observational replications in settings with heavy early uptake.

The SW materials summarize the RCT evidence as extremely strong against severe COVID: Pfizer was 9:1 and Moderna 30:0, which the brief translates to about 97.5% efficacy against severe disease in that endpoint. The same packet also points to the first Israeli observational study as closely reproducing the trial shape and estimating roughly 92% reduction in severe COVID.

Hong Kong is especially relevant because it was initially close to an infection-naive setting and still found strong protection against severe outcomes and death, including about 89.3% effectiveness for two doses in adults 60+ and about 97.9% for three doses against severe or fatal outcomes.

Causal interpretation: for the part of the pandemic when COVID mortality was concentrated in older adults, these are not trivial or ambiguous signals. They support the proposition that vaccination likely prevented substantial severe disease and some deaths.

Caveat: these sources do not directly prove the exact total number of lives saved in the United States through end-2022, and they do not eliminate all observational bias. But they are materially stronger than the negative side's most fragile ecological screens.

What would weaken this claim: a transparent US age-stratified no-vaccine counterfactual, with proper negative controls, showing no meaningful reduction in severe COVID or death in the highest-risk groups.

## Claim 2: The Main Negative Objections Are Real But Not Decisive

HVE and NCACM are serious issues. The Hoeg/Arbel exchange is a good example of why a single observational estimate can be distorted by non-COVID mortality differences. But that critique weakens one study; it does not invalidate all of the direct efficacy evidence above, and it does not by itself establish net harm.

The Florida Levi/Ladapo study is also not a clean no-vaccine counterfactual. It is an active-comparator brand study among vaccinated people. That makes it useful as a signal, but it still leaves room for residual confounding, especially around product choice, timing, frailty, and access. A Pfizer-vs-Moderna difference is not the same thing as proving that vaccination versus no vaccination increased total US deaths.

The persistent elevation in US all-cause mortality is important, but it is not a vaccine-specific fingerprint. It is compatible with several competing explanations: COVID wave timing, delayed care, chronic disease burden, age structure, coding, and other pandemic-era harms. The key point is not that the elevation is irrelevant; it is that the elevation is not, by itself, a clean attribution to vaccine harm.

Causal interpretation: these objections lower confidence in precise effect sizes, but they do not flip the direction of the best-supported evidence.

Caveat: if the Florida signal replicated in a stricter design, or if a better NCACM-negative-control study showed the same brand difference with no plausible bias explanation, that would be a meaningful update against my side.

What would weaken this claim: a properly controlled US active-comparator or record-level study showing the same mortality pattern with much tighter bias control, plus a coherent mechanism that fits the broader mortality record.

## Claim 3: The Population-Level Record Fits Benefit Better Than Large Net Harm

The negative side relies heavily on population screens, but those screens are underidentified. OWID slope plots, cross-country slope comparisons, and similar ecological figures are not clean estimates of total vaccine benefit. They are useful as falsification screens, not as final causal adjudication.

The same is true of persistent cross-country excess mortality. Mostert-like findings show that mortality stayed elevated in many places despite vaccination, but they do not identify vaccination as the cause. They show that the pandemic period remained deadly; they do not quantify the no-vaccine counterfactual.

That matters because the burden on the negative side is not merely to point to excess mortality. It is to show that the excess is best explained by vaccination rather than by the many other pandemic-era forces already known to affect mortality. So far, the ecological screens do not do that job cleanly.

Causal interpretation: if large net vaccine harm were present, I would expect a clearer, more stable population fingerprint than these screens provide. What I see instead is mixed, variant-dependent, country-dependent mortality with strong confounding pressure.

Caveat: this does not prove zero harm. It means the population-level measurement problem is still too noisy to support a large net-harm conclusion over the competing direct efficacy evidence.

What would weaken this claim: a pre-registered, transparently specified analysis that predicts and then reproduces a strong downward population fingerprint for large net benefit, or an equally clean upward fingerprint tying excess mortality specifically to vaccination rather than to pandemic timing.

## Bottom Line

The negative side has identified real uncertainties, but it has not yet produced a cleaner causal package than the pro-benefit side. The best direct evidence still supports meaningful protection against severe COVID, and the objections to that evidence are mostly the kinds of confounding problems that make exact attribution hard, not the kinds that decisively reverse the sign.

So under a normal civil standard, the more defensible conclusion remains that mRNA vaccination likely net saved lives through the end of 2022, even if the exact size of the benefit is uncertain and even if some observational mortality estimates are badly biased.
