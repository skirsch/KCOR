## Verdict

No winner / unresolved.

Under a 55% civil-standard threshold, neither side cleared the bar on the actual question of net US lives saved versus cost lives through the end of 2022. The judge found that Saar had the stronger direct-efficacy story, but Kirsch had the stronger reconciliation story, and neither packet closed the gap cleanly enough to award a winner.

## Scorecard

- Direct efficacy evidence: Saar.
- Causal validity of observational VE-death estimates: Kirsch.
- Population-level benefit fingerprint: Kirsch.
- US-specific harm evidence: Kirsch.
- US net-mortality reconciliation: Kirsch.
- Counterfactual completeness: mixed / unresolved.

## Decisive Reasons

1. Saar's packet establishes real short-term severe-COVID protection, especially from RCT and early observational material, so this is not a clean Kirsch sweep.
2. Saar's case still leans heavily on modeled lives-saved estimates, and those do not by themselves reconcile the persistent US all-cause mortality elevation in the UCOD summaries.
3. Kirsch's best objections are not knockdowns of efficacy, but they meaningfully weaken confidence in observational VE-death estimates because the HVE/NCACM problem is real and repeatedly undercontrolled.
4. The OWID slope screens do not show the clean downward population fingerprint one would expect from a large, durable benefit, but they are not strong enough on their own to prove net harm.
5. Kirsch's US harm signals are serious enough to keep the question open, but still not clean enough to prove total net cost of lives.

## Process Note

The judge found that the new category framing materially improved the process. It separated direct efficacy from observational validity, population fingerprint, harm evidence, and US reconciliation. This clarified that the core unresolved issue is not "did vaccines have any efficacy?" but "can that efficacy be translated into a convincing US net-mortality advantage through the end of 2022?"
