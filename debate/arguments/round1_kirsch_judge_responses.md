# Round 1 Kirsch Judge Responses

These responses are limited to the judge questions that matter most for the Kirsch side.

## 3. Smallest Plausible Vaccine-Attributable ACM Effect

I cannot responsibly turn the Levi/Ladapo Florida brand-comparison study into a precise US death count. It is an active-comparator mRNA study, not a no-vaccine counterfactual, so it does not identify total vaccine-attributable ACM on its own.

What I *can* say is that the cleanest conservative reading is **nonzero NCACM harm, not zero**. The point of the Florida result is not that it proves a large population death toll by itself, but that it makes "there is no meaningful mortality downside" too strong. Even after taking the usual confounding objections seriously, the result still leaves a residual signal that is hard to dismiss as pure noise.

If I am forced to express that as an effect size, my claim is a **modest but nonzero excess NCACM effect** that survives worst-case confounding. I would not pretend that Levi/Ladapo identifies the exact causal magnitude; I would use it as a floor on harm, not a final point estimate. In practical terms, the study supports the claim that the true ACM effect is not cleanly zero and is plausibly in the **single-digit to low-double-digit percent excess range**, but the exact percentage remains unresolved.

The burden on Saar is to show why this strictly matched Pfizer-vs-Moderna signal is fully explained away, and to do so with a better US active-comparator design, not with general statements about observational bias.

## 4. The Population-Level Fingerprint That Should Have Shown Up

If large net benefit were real by late 2022, I would have expected a **downward population fingerprint** that was hard to miss in the mortality record. The simplest pre-specified version is:

- high-vaccination settings should show a visibly lower cumulative COVID-death slope than low-vaccination settings after rollout, and
- that separation should widen over time rather than remain nearly parallel.

In other words, if vaccination was saving many lives at scale, the OWID slope screens should have shown a clear downward break in the high-coverage countries, not nearly parallel wave-slope trends. Likewise, the US mortality record should have moved back toward baseline more clearly than it did.

My concrete expectation is not a narrative but a direction: **the fitted slope trend in the high-vaccination group should have been lower, not flat or upward relative to the low-vaccination group**. The fact that the all-country screen and the vaccination-contrast screens do not show an obvious downward fingerprint is exactly why the large-net-benefit claim remains unproven.

## Short Burden Note For Saar

If Saar wants to say net benefit still clearly wins, he needs to reconcile two things quantitatively:

1. the persistent US mortality elevation in the UCOD summaries, and
2. the absence of a strong downward population fingerprint in the OWID slope screens.

Without that reconciliation, the pro-benefit case is still model-heavy and measurement-light.
